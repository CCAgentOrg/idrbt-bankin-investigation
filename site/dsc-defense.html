Counter-Argument Guide

Red Teaming the "DSC Defense"

IDRBT may argue that physical DSC tokens make the breach harmless. This page prepares the counter-argument — explaining each scenario in plain language so anyone can understand why this defense fails.

CashlessConsumer  ·  June 29, 2026

What This Page Is

When the IDRBT portal was found to have 33+ unauthenticated API endpoints exposing the credentials of 5,576 bank employees, a predictable defense emerged: "No real harm was done because you still need a physical Digital Signature Certificate (DSC) token — a USB dongle — to actually change any domain records."

This page red-teams that argument — meaning it looks for every possible way the defense is wrong, weak, or incomplete. Think of it as preparing for a debate: here are the points IDRBT will likely make, and here's why each one fails.

⚠️ The short version: The DSC defense focuses on one narrow question (can you change DNS records directly from the internet?) while ignoring 7 other attack paths that don't need a DSC at all. It's like a bank claiming "our vault is secure" while the front door, the windows, the roof, and the staff entrance are all wide open.

Each scenario below explains what happened (the technical fact), what it means (in everyday language), and why the DSC defense doesn't cover it.

SCENARIO 1

The DSC Proxy Was Already Exposed

🎯 In Simple Terms

Imagine the vault door has a special lock that needs a physical key (the DSC token). That's what IDRBT will say protects the system. But here's the thing: the machine that controls the lock was itself left unlocked and accessible from the internet. Anyone could walk up to it and ask it to do things — without ever touching the physical key.

What Was Found

The portal's DSC middleware — the software that talks to the physical DSC tokens — had four publicly accessible endpoints that required zero authentication. No login, no password, no session. These were proxied through IDRBT's Apache server to the eMudhra DSC middleware running at https://localhost.emudhra.com:26769.

EndpointWhat It DoesAuth Required?
/dsc/getTokenRequestStarts a DSC token listing sessionNone
/dsc/getTokenList?data=XLists available DSC tokens by nameNone
/dsc/getCertificateRequestRequests certificate listing for a tokenNone
/dsc/getSigningRequestRequests a DSC signing operationNone
🔍 Story Time: The Locksmith's Workshop

Imagine a bank says: "Our vault can only be opened with a physical key held by authorized staff." That sounds secure — until you discover that the locksmith's workshop (the DSC middleware) has a window anyone can climb through. The workshop has tools that can cast new keys, authorize new users, and even run the vault's opening mechanism. The only thing protecting the workshop's tools is a simple code written on a piece of paper — and if you can break that code, you can order the vault to open itself from the workshop, without ever touching the actual key.

The Weakness

The encryption protecting the DSC payload was the only barrier. But:

  • The encryption key ID is returned in the same unauthenticated response as the encrypted data
  • The same server had multiple other security failures (CORS wildcard, actuator leaks, .env exposure)
  • The same eMudhra middleware likely uses the same encryption keys across all environments

If the encryption can be cracked — and in badly-managed systems, encryption keys are often weak, shared, or default — an attacker could initiate DSC signing sessions remotely. No physical token needed.

🔴 The unanswered question: Was the DSC middleware's encryption ever independently audited for key strength, rotation, and isolation? If nobody checked, then the only thing between an unauthenticated attacker and remote DSC signing was a layer of encryption whose strength IDRBT cannot prove.

SCENARIO 2

DSC Does Not Gate All Portal Actions

🎯 In Simple Terms

Even if we accept that the DSC perfectly protects DNS changes (which Scenario 1 shows it doesn't), the DSC does nothing to protect all the other things you can do on the portal. It's like saying "my front door has a good lock" while ignoring that the windows are open, the garage door is up, and the dog door is big enough to crawl through.

2a — Billing & Invoice Manipulation

The billing endpoints (/api/dr/invoice/*) exposed 1,535 invoice records worth ₹4.72 crore — including bank staff names, emails, and phone numbers. DSC has nothing to do with billing. An attacker with portal access could:

  • Change billing contact info for any bank's domain
  • Redirect invoice emails to their own address
  • Learn payment patterns to time attacks
  • Let domains expire by manipulating renewal dates
💰 Story Time: The Paperwork Trap

An attacker doesn't need to crack the vault to cause damage. They just change the billing address on the utility bills. Now the bank stops receiving invoices, misses renewal deadlines, and its .bank.in domain expires. Suddenly the bank's website goes offline — or worse, someone else registers the domain. All without touching the DSC.

2b — Account Takeover & Privilege Escalation

The portal had 1,072 orphan Super Admin accounts — accounts with no organization attached, meaning they can modify any bank's domain settings. These accounts default to the highest privilege on registration. An attacker who cracks one account's password (bcrypt hashes can be cracked offline) can:

  • Create new Super Admin accounts
  • Modify other users' roles
  • View every bank registration
  • Initiate domain transfer requests that an unwitting DSC holder might approve
🔑 The key insight: The attack is not "change DNS directly." The attack is "become the person who requests the DSC signature." If you're a Super Admin on the portal, you don't need to bypass the DSC — you work with it, becoming the authorized requester.

2c — System Configuration Exposed

The static config endpoint (/api/dr/static/getAll) exposed domain pricing, GST rates, payment gateway details, and email validation rules. An attacker could use this to craft payment interception attacks or understand the full system layout.

SCENARIO 3

The Physical DSC Assumption Is Weak

🎯 In Simple Terms

IDRBT's defense assumes DSC tokens are always used perfectly — held securely by authorized staff, PINs kept secret, never shared. But real-world banking — especially in smaller cooperative banks in India — doesn't work like that. The evidence from the investigation itself shows a pattern of security shortcuts.

3a — DSC Use in Indian Cooperative Banks

In a typical Indian cooperative bank:

  • The branch manager and the "IT person" (often the same person) share a small office
  • The DSC token sits permanently in the USB port of a shared computer
  • The PIN is written on a sticky note under the keyboard — or worse, it's the default set by the vendor
  • When the token is needed and the authorized person is on leave, the PIN is shared over WhatsApp
🏦 Story Time: The Shared USB Port

"Sir, the portal is asking for the DSC. Can you give the PIN?" — this WhatsApp message, sent from one bank employee to another, is the real-world security posture for thousands of cooperative banks in India. The investigation found 1,395 personal email logins (Gmail, Yahoo, Rediffmail) on the national banking registry. If bank staff use personal emails for something as critical as domain management, they almost certainly share DSC tokens informally too.

EvidenceWhat It Suggests
1,395 personal email loginsSecurity culture is informal; DSC tokens likely shared too
1,072 orphan Super Admin accountsHighest privilege assigned by default; DSC management likely follows the same lax default
43 ghost accounts — non-bank orgs with portal accessWho verified these organizations' DSC holders?
Test domains (VKTEST, IDTMAY) with live SSL certsIf test domains get real certificates, test DSC tokens may also exist in production

3b — DSC PINs Are Crackable

Hardware DSC tokens are protected by a 4-6 digit PIN. Common PINs in Indian banking include:

  • 1234, 0000 — factory default, never changed
  • The bank's IFSC code (publicly known)
  • The employee's birth date (often available on social media)
  • A shared PIN written on paper in the drawer

The investigation exposed 5,576 employee records with names, email addresses, phone numbers, and device fingerprints. With this data, social engineering becomes laser-targeted: "Hello Mr. Patel, I'm calling from IDRBT support. We need to verify your DSC PIN for a security update."

3c — Remote Signing Architecture Exists

The eMudhra DSC middleware runs as a network service — the portal's frontend makes HTTP calls to it. This architecture inherently supports network-attached DSC operations. The question is not "can you sign remotely?" but "how well is the middleware protected?"

SCENARIO 4

The "Change DNS" Assumption Is Wrong

🎯 In Simple Terms

The DSC defense assumes the only bad thing that can happen is someone changes your bank's address in the phonebook. But there are much scarier things an attacker can do that don't involve changing the address at all. They can create a copy of your bank's address that looks identical — and redirect customers there.

4a — The Perfect Phishing Machine

Here's a step-by-step scenario that requires only portal access (via cracked credentials from Scenario 2):

  1. Hijack the domain's DNS: Change the A record for sirsadccb.bank.in (a real cooperative bank) to point to a server the attacker controls.
  2. Get a free SSL certificate: Let's Encrypt issues DV certificates automatically — no identity check, takes 30 seconds. Now the fake site shows a green padlock 🔒.
  3. The browser trusts it: The URL bar says sirsadccb.bank.in — because the DNS change propagated. The padlock is green — because the SSL certificate is cryptographically valid.
  4. The customer can't tell: They see the familiar bank logo, the .bank.in domain they trust, and the reassuring padlock. They enter their username, password, and OTP.
  5. The account is drained.
🌐 Story Time: The Perfect Fake

Imagine a scammer puts up a fake storefront that looks exactly like your bank — same logo, same layout, same everything. And they put it on the same street where your bank is supposed to be (the .bank.in domain). And the city government put up the correct street sign (the green padlock). You walk in, hand over your money, and only realize later that it wasn't the real bank. The .bank.in TLD was supposed to prevent exactly this — but without DNSSEC, the attacker can redirect your traffic to their fake server without ever touching the registry.

⚠️ Key difference from global .bank: The global .bank TLD mandates Extended Validation (EV) or Organization Validation (OV) certificates — these require real identity verification. But .bank.in accepts Domain Validation (DV) certificates — the same automated, no-ID-check type used by phishing sites. A .bank.in phishing site gets the same browser trust indicators as the real bank.

4b — Does Changing DNS Even Require DSC?

Looking at the Angular source code of the portal:

  • The DSC proxy is described as being "used to sign domain registration undertakings" — not routine DNS management
  • DNS record management (A, AAAA, CNAME, MX, NS records) likely uses a different authorization workflow
  • Billing, contact info, and account management almost certainly do NOT require DSC

If DNS record changes don't require DSC, the entire defense collapses. An attacker with portal credentials can redirect banking traffic directly.

SCENARIO 5

The IKCON Insider Threat

🎯 In Simple Terms

It doesn't matter how good the lock is if the people who built the lock have a master key. IKCON Technologies — the company that built the IDRBT portal — had 22 employee accounts on the system, including 3 with global Super Admin access. That's like giving the construction crew the keys to every room in the bank, forever.

5a — Vendor Super Admin Access

IKCON employees with Super Admin access can:

  • Modify any bank's domain settings — including DNS records
  • Access the DSC middleware configuration — bypassing the "physical token" entirely
  • View all user data, invoices, and system configuration
  • Create new accounts with any role
  • Modify the application itself through portal infrastructure
🔴 Physical DSC is irrelevant here. IKCON has system-level access that bypasses the DSC workflow entirely. The question is not "can they change DNS?" — they have root-level access to the system that manages DNS for every bank in India.

5b — No Tender, No Oversight

As documented in the investigation:

  • No public tender — No RFP, no competitive bidding, no published contract award
  • No banking security expertise — IKCON's core business is video conferencing and ticketing systems, not banking domain security
  • No access audit — 22 accounts with 3 Super Admins, no evidence of separation of duties or periodic access review
  • No competitive process — In violation of IDRBT's own procurement handbook
🏗️ Story Time: The Builder's Keys

You hire a company to build a bank vault. They do a decent job. But then you give them permanent keys to every part of the bank — all safes, all lockers, all management systems. And you don't check what they're doing with those keys. You never required a competitive bid, so you don't even know if they were the best company for the job. Their main business is building conference rooms, not vaults. And you have no logs of who enters the bank after hours. This is not security. This is trust — blind trust.

SCENARIO 6

DNSSEC & DMARC Irrelevance

🎯 In Simple Terms

This is the most important scenario for non-tech readers. Even if we accept that the DSC perfectly protects the registry (which Scenarios 1-5 show it doesn't), attackers don't need to attack the registry. They can attack the phonebook lookup itself — fooling the internet into sending banking traffic to the wrong address. And .bank.in has no protection against this.

6a — The Phonebook Problem (DNSSEC)

When you type sirsadccb.bank.in in your browser, your computer asks the internet's phonebook (DNS): "Where is this bank's website?" The phonebook says: "It's at IP address X."

Without DNSSEC, your computer has no way to verify that the answer is genuine. An attacker can:

  • DNS cache poisoning: Trick your internet provider's phonebook into remembering the wrong address
  • BGP hijacking: Announce the bank's IP range from a different country — big chunks of the internet will believe it
  • Compromise the hosting provider: 4+ cooperative banks are hosted on shared $5/month servers in the US — if that server is compromised, all domains on it are compromised
📊 80% of cooperative banks have no DNSSEC. The DSC defense says "we protect the phonebook's master copy." The question is whether anyone verifies that the copies people receive are authentic. For 80% of cooperative banks, the answer is no — and you don't need to change the master copy to exploit this.

6b — The Email Forgery Problem (DMARC)

Without DMARC, anyone can send an email that appears to come from sirsadccb.bank.in. The email will land in the recipient's inbox. Combined with the exposed employee contact details:

📧 Story Time: The Fake Email

You get an email that appears to be from your bank. The sender address is support@sirsadccb.bank.in. The email says your account needs verification and includes a link. The link leads to a page that looks exactly like your bank's login page. You enter your credentials. The attacker now has them. All of this works without a single DNS change, without a DSC token, without any portal access — just a misconfigured DMARC record on the bank's domain.

📊 40% of cooperative banks have no DMARC. This means no email authentication. Any attacker can impersonate any of these banks via email. This is a fundamental security failure that the DSC doesn't even attempt to address.
80%of cooperative banks lack DNSSEC — DNS responses can be faked
40%of cooperative banks lack DMARC — emails can be forged
47%of banks lack HSTS — connections downgradable to HTTP
95+domains have no nameservers — registered but not accessible

SCENARIO 7

The "No Exploit Code" Counter-Argument

🎯 In Simple Terms

IDRBT might also say: "No evidence of actual exploitation was found. The vulnerability was discovered by a researcher, not by a hacker." This sounds reassuring — but it's actually meaningless. Think of it this way: if you left your house unlocked for a year and nobody stole anything, does that mean your house was secure? Or does it just mean you were lucky?

The Vulnerability Was Live for 13+ Months

The portal launched in May 2025. The vulnerabilities existed continuously until June 2026. In that time:

  • The API was trivially discoverable — anyone running a subdomain scanner or reading Certificate Transparency logs would find the Angular bundle paths
  • The portal had no security monitoring — the exposed config showed no logging infrastructure. If IDRBT wasn't logging API access, they cannot prove no malicious access occurred
  • The portal had no vulnerability disclosure program — no security.txt, no bug bounty, no way for researchers to report issues officially
  • CERT-In did not issue a public advisory — affected banks were not notified and cannot check their own logs for suspicious activity
⚖️ You cannot assert "no harm occurred" when you had no ability to detect harm. The absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. This is a fundamental principle of security — and a courtroom principle too.

The Data Theft Itself Is the Breach

Even if no DNS changes were made (which we cannot verify), the investigation found that:

  • 5,576 user records were exposed — names, emails, phones, device fingerprints
  • 6,752 bcrypt password hashes were extractable
  • 1,535 invoices with bank staff details were readable

This data is a permanent intelligence resource. Every bank employee on that list can now be targeted for phishing, credential stuffing, and social engineering — for years to come. The breach is not measured by what did happen, but by what the exposed data enables.

Summary: Why the DSC Defense Fails

🎯 The One-Line Takeaway

The DSC defense takes one specific attack path (directly changing DNS records through the API) and claims that because that path is protected, the overall breach is insignificant. This ignores that there are at least 7 other attack paths — and most of them don't need a DSC at all.

IDRBT ClaimWhy It Fails
"DSC prevents remote DNS changes"The DSC proxy itself was exposed (4 unauthenticated endpoints). If the middleware's encryption is weak, the DSC is bypassed entirely.
"No actual DNS changes were made"The data exfiltration — 5,576 user records, 6,752 password hashes, 1,535 invoices — is itself a completed breach.
"Physical DSC is required"Cooperative bank DSC practices are weak (shared PINs, sticky notes). IKCON has Super Admin access that bypasses all DSC controls.
"The impact is limited to the portal"Exposed data enables targeted phishing, credential stuffing on other banking systems, and a permanent intelligence resource.
"It's been patched now"The endpoints were exposed for 13+ months. The patch only covered 7 monitored endpoints — the full 33+ list needs verification.
"DSC signing requires physical token"The portal's own architecture shows DSC can be network-attached through the eMudhra middleware. "Physical token" does not mean "physically secure signing."
🏁 Bottom line: In security, you do not measure a breach by the narrowest possible interpretation of exploitability. You measure it by the data exposed, the attack paths enabled, and the duration of exposure. By all three measures, this breach is critical — regardless of where the physical DSC tokens were sitting.
